Speaking Writing Now
I work at the intersection of design and institutional power. Most of my career has been spent figuring out how organizations actually function, why their systems break down, and what it takes to build something better.

People get confused looking at my background because it doesn't fit neat categories. I've led design teams at federal agencies, taught at universities, coached high school tennis, and organized design communities across multiple cities. But there's a through-line: I'm interested in how systems work, how they fail, and how technology gets deployed to either strengthen or fracture social bonds.

Most recently I was at 18F, where I scaled design practices across federal agencies and led digital transformation efforts affecting millions of people. These days I teach at the University of Michigan, research how platforms disrupt public trust, and work on projects that examine the intentional engineering of systemic failure.

My work spans service design, organizational change, and critical research. I've built design capabilities in environments where traditional approaches don't work—places with legacy systems, complex stakeholder relationships, and real constraints. I'm particularly interested in how design leadership functions in institutional settings and what it takes to create sustainable change in bureaucratic environments.

Current Work

I'm exploring how technology is engineered to concentrate power and fragment collective action. This research happens through exhibitions, speculative projects, and academic work that examines the mechanics of institutional breakdown.

I also organize design communities, including Portland Design Month and various civic tech initiatives. I believe in building connections between practitioners working in mission-critical environments.
