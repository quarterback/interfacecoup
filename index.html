Interface Control Research

Speculative Critical Design

A speculative design project examining how technological interfaces reshape power, policy, and social systems

Project Overview

This research explores how technological systems are reshaping societal power structures through interface control. It examines the gap between rapid technological change and slower policy adaptation, revealing how this mismatch creates new forms of control and system vulnerability.

Critical Issues

  • → Technology enables changes that outpace policy mechanisms, creating governance gaps
  • → Administrative systems become dependent on interfaces they don't control
  • → Financialization through technology creates new forms of system vulnerability
  • → Communities lose capacity as they become dependent on private platforms

Research Approach

This project combines multiple analytical frameworks to understand system change:

  • → Mapping how interface control reshapes administrative capacity
  • → Documenting patterns of system failure and crisis cascades
  • → Analyzing how financialization creates new dependencies
  • → Identifying points of intervention and system resilience

Critical Infrastructure Glossary

Core Concepts

Interface Coup

The seizure of power through control of administrative interfaces rather than physical force. A process where organizations lose operational independence by becoming dependent on technical systems they don't control.

Administrative Violence

The use of bureaucratic tools and technical systems as mechanisms of control. Converting administrative processes into control points that make resistance procedurally impossible.

Platform Parasitism

The process of degrading community capacity while creating platform dependencies. Involves destroying local infrastructure while making communities dependent on private platforms.

Patterns

Crisis Cascade

A pattern where system failures compound and accelerate each other. Each failure makes the next more likely, creating accelerating cycles of degradation.

Manufactured Inefficiency

The deliberate creation of system inefficiencies to enable control and extraction. Making systems complex enough to require specialized interfaces for basic operation.

Interface Lockout

When control of technical interfaces becomes de facto control of entire systems. The process where interface access determines operational capability.

Systems

Boring Systems

Critical but undervalued administrative systems that enable modern stability. The invisible infrastructure of daily life that becomes vulnerable through its own success.

Knowledge Sovereignty

The ability of communities to maintain and control their own knowledge systems. Often undermined through technical dependencies and platform capture.
